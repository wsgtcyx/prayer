## üß¨ Discovery

The spade-toothed beaked whale was first identified from a lower jaw with teeth discovered by Henry Travers on Pitt Island in 1872. This find was reported in 1873 by James Hector, who initially referred it to *Mesoplodon layardii*. The species was later described as a new species by John Edward Gray in 1874, who named it in honor of Travers. 

In December 2010, two specimens, a mother and calf, were found stranded on Opape Beach in the Bay of Plenty, New Zealand. Initially mistaken for Gray's beaked whales, genetic analysis later revealed them to be the first complete specimens of the spade-toothed whale. This discovery provided the first opportunity to study the external appearance of the species.

The most recent significant discovery occurred on July 4, 2024, when a deceased intact male spade-toothed whale washed ashore near Taieri Mouth on the southern east coast of South Island, New Zealand. This specimen, being particularly fresh, has offered scientists a rare chance to study the physical characteristics and gather more data on this elusive species.

Spade-toothed beaked whales face significant threats from noise pollution, particularly from naval sonar and seismic activity. These deep-water species are highly sensitive to underwater noise, which can disrupt their natural behaviors, such as communication, navigation, and feeding. The intense sound waves from sonar and seismic surveys can cause physical harm or disorientation, leading to strandings and fatalities.

Another major threat to spade-toothed beaked whales is bycatch, which occurs when these whales become unintentionally entangled in fishing gear. This is a common issue for many marine species, but it is particularly detrimental for the spade-toothed beaked whale due to its rarity. Entanglement can lead to injury, impaired movement, and even death, further reducing the already limited population of this elusive species.

## üåç Conservation Efforts

Conservationists are urgently advocating for increased protection measures for the spade-toothed whale, one of the world's rarest and least understood marine mammals. The primary focus is on implementing stricter regulations on naval activities and fishing practices within their known habitats to mitigate the threats posed by human activities.

Noise pollution, particularly from naval sonar and seismic activities, poses a significant threat to the spade-toothed whale. Conservationists are calling for the enforcement of quieter naval operations and the establishment of marine protected areas where such activities are restricted.

Bycatch, the unintentional capture of non-target species in fishing gear, is another critical threat to the spade-toothed whale. Conservation efforts are pushing for the adoption of more selective fishing techniques and the use of whale-safe gear to reduce the risk of entanglement. These measures are essential to prevent further decline of this already elusive species.

Given the spade-toothed whale's 'Data Deficient' status on the IUCN Red List, there is a pressing need for more research to gather comprehensive data on their population, distribution, and behavior.
